Studies

ACES

The Adolescent Communication of Emotion Study (ACES) is seeking to granularly measure & quantify social and emotional behavior in children ages 12-17. Advances in technology have made it possible to use computer vision and computational linguistics to identify biobehavioral markers of autism spectrum disorder (ASD), including ones that were identified in the EAVE study. The ACES study is using the NIH Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework to guide development and validity testing of said markers, to establish measures of social and emotional behavior.

Questions? Reach out to acesstudy@chop.edu

EAVE

Sponsored by the Eagles Autism Foundation, EAVE seeks to use the RDoC framework to study co-occurring disorders of ASD and to differentiate between overlapping symptoms across diagnoses. Participants ages 12-17 and with an ASD diagnosis will be measured over three time points, with one in-person and two virtual visits. Biobehavioral markers of ASD symptoms, as well as co-occurring symptoms, will be established by measuring emotional facial expressions, heart rate variability, and responses to clinical measures.

SDS

The goal of the SDS grant is to develop a Sensor Data Standard, a structure for organizing and storing data obtained from sensors (i.e. audio, video, etc). This framework is designed based on BIDS, a similar standard made for MRI imaging data. This grant will also see the development of an authenticator that will check if selected data are in compliance with the standard. SDS will be made publicly available via a GitHub page or similar.
